Surety Bonds In-Depth

Learn how standard surety bonds operate, who qualifies as a cosigner, and what obligations are created under a standard contract.

What is a Surety Bond?

A surety bond is a contract between the court, the bail bond agency (surety), and the defendant's cosigner (obligee). The bail bondsman guarantees to the court that the defendant will attend all trials and hearings. In return, the cosigner pays a state-regulated premium (normally 10% of the total bail amount) and provides security/collateral to back this promise.

If the defendant appears in court as scheduled, the bond is dissolved upon trial completion, and any collateral is released. If the defendant misses court, the surety company assumes the liability to locate them or forfeit the full bail amount to the state.

Qualifications for a Surety Bond Cosigner

To qualify as a guarantor or cosigner for a surety bail bond, you must meet the following criteria:

  • Must be a US citizen or legal permanent resident.
  • Must have verifiable, stable employment or steady income.
  • Must present a government-issued photo ID and proof of residency.
  • Must possess assets or credit status sufficient to cover potential bond forfeitures.

Your Rights as a Cosigner

Cosigners have specific legal protections under our agency policy:

  • Right to withdraw as a guarantor if you believe the defendant will flee (conditions apply).
  • Right to view all court documents, scheduling logs, and bond filings.
  • Right to request immediate collateral audits once court certificates of disposition are filed.

Understand the Financial Liabilities

Please note these crucial risk notices before executing agreements:

If the defendant flees state jurisdiction, you are liable for the full face value of the bail bond, not just the 10% premium. Recovering a defendant who skips court may also incur capture fees which are charged to the cosigner.
